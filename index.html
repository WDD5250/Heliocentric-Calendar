Overview

The Heliocentric Calendar is a proposed reform of the Gregorian calendar designed to align civil timekeeping with the astronomical events that define Earth's relationship to the Sun. Unlike the Gregorian calendar, whose month boundaries have no correspondence to any celestial phenomenon, the Heliocentric Calendar anchors each quarter directly to a solstice or equinox.

The calendar preserves the familiar structure of twelve months and the seven-day week, ensuring straightforward civil adoption, while correcting several longstanding deficiencies of the inherited Roman–Gregorian system.

Structure

The year begins on the March equinox (approximately Gregorian March 20). The first quarter (March–May, 31 days each) opens at the March equinox; the second (June–August, 31 days each) at the June solstice; the third (September–November, 30 days each) at the September equinox; and the fourth (December–February, 30/30/29 days) at the December solstice. The unequal quarter lengths (93/93/90/89) reflect the genuine asymmetry of Earth's orbit around the Sun.

The Epoch and Year Notation

The calendar corrects the absence of a Year Zero. The epoch year is denoted 0 E (Epocham). Years prior are written with A.E. (Ante Epocham); years following use P.E. (Post Epocham), omitted in everyday use just as A.D. is conventionally dropped.

3 A.E. → 2 A.E. → 1 A.E. → 0 E → 1 P.E. → 2 P.E. replaces 3 B.C. → 1 B.C. → 1 A.D. → 2 A.D. — eliminating the missing year and the sign-inversion problem.

The Leap Year Rule

A Heliocentric year Y is a leap year if and only if Gregorian year Y+1 is a leap year (divisible by 4, except centennial years, unless divisible by 400). The intercalary day is February 30, corresponding to Gregorian March 19.

Why This Reform

The Gregorian calendar encodes millennia of historical accident: months of arbitrary length, a year beginning in January for no astronomical reason, a missing Year Zero, and quarter boundaries unrelated to the solstices they nominally reference. The Heliocentric Calendar addresses each deficiency while preserving the week, twelve-month structure, and existing epoch to make civil transition feasible.
